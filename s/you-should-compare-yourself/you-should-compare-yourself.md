# You should compare yourself to others

I tend to not even notice who wrote the article (or even message on a board) that I'm currently reading, as the message is far more important than the messenger.
There are two effects from this:
- I might consider new ideas without any previous assumptions, and be open to new ideas even if they come from people I would consider opposed to my beliefs.
- I tend to dismiss the current takes by people who got fame for previous achievments, and that are now serving recycled takes - that some people instantly start to reconsider after these specific persons weighed-in.

Sometimes, I even connect the dots months or years later, and realize some piece I've been reading has been written by someone present in an online space, either X/Slack/Discord...

There is something that is quite particular in the end-result for me: the iternet somehow feels like a single entity.

I feel dreaded at my lack of specific achievments, now being 30 years old.
I don't think this is the result of low cognitive capabilities but of lack of focus - and this is actually what drives me to write this, in a very selfish way, as I recently started perceiving the value of this activity to structure my stream of thoughts.
Having so much value being put-out is overwhelming - even if not from a single person I care to remember.

---

If I look at my parents and uncles, the PhD seems to be the norm, mostly in scientific fields.
Yet I had the luck of observing them behaving in the most stupid ways sometimes.

---

The earliest memory I have of one of my uncles is me reciting the alphabet. As soone as I finished, he goes like: "Great! Now, backwards!"

There's also a more recent memory of another uncle, this one on my mother's side, that we had dinner with. As I was paraphrasing one of the last research my father was conducting "he did this, then that, then...", he starts squinting his eyes, slightly raising his head... I don't have the time to finish thinking "what the fuck" that he begins his sentence:
"You know... You're father is not really intelligent."
Obviously I just burst out in laughter.

Sometimes it's less about competition, it's not necessarily about the hyper-focused computer imaging expert jusging the graph theory scientist. That actually spends more time implementing algorithms from other people's research rather than researching his own - that would make him nothing more than an engineer, could we tolerate this?
Sometimes it's just a subtle enforcing of the norm.

When I told my mother I would probably not try to go for a PhD after my master's degree, I remember her exact reaction (leaving as-is for my french readers):
"Rooooh, tu vas faire un doctorat, Charles, quand-mÃªme!"
"Ohhh, you're going to do a PhD, Charles, come on!" - sorry it's not the same without that gutural french sound, you're definitely missing something there!

I had to convince her for 2 or 3 minutes that it made sense (not that I'm not currently reconsidering that past choice...), as it doesn't open that much doors in France for what I wanted to do, compared to the regular Master's degree.
Her reaction was wholesome, she wished the best for me, but it was still akin to learning your child would go to a professional school before turning 18.

---

One of the most key realization moment didn't come from family but a close friend I respected immensely for his writings.
While philosophy is his core interest, he is still technical, as being the smart kid being born mid 90s tend to push you toward computers.
I helped him with his React code for handling reactive components, his js code for physics simulations (in js as performance was not a limiting factor, these were learning oriented), and his Ghost.js instance.
But that wasn't what helped the most - I knew that while he was far better than average, while investing so little time compared to anyone who actually gained knowledge through time considered "professional", it wasn't what he was supposed to be good at, and I was the one helping him only on the things that were my things, and not his things.
What actually helped the most was inviting him to the coworking space I sometimes hanged at, and seeing him work on his thing.
I saw the white page, I saw the need to rewrite multiple times, I saw the hesitations...

And this doesn't dismiss his overall acomplishments. I don't need to see people struggle as a way to cope with my inability to deliver something meaningful.
I need to see people struggling as a way to reassure myself that the people I have a profound respect for their ability to deliver also have these moments, and that, actually, helps the most at not feeling stuck and worthless when I too have those moments.
Everyone have those. The only people that somehow don't have those are the ones that are so used at producing content that it is now somehow automatic. They had years to experiment and make it seem seamless.

---

Unfortunately I now also ponder about the lack of consistency towards my intellectual pursuits.
It is true that adaptation to the other norms, the ones of the people I didn't choose to be around, made me less able to express special interests, and I sometimes wish I was just able to let go and not try to fit.
But even as I'm reflecting on this more and more in the recent years, and cutting some connections made a lot more time available for growth, I still had to try to understand the lack of one or two definitive "great things" that I should (according to the high bar that was set earlier in my life) have been able to produce.
I always shined in environments where the urgencies were clearly defined, as it was always easier not to jump on the next toy project.
Seeing friends failing or getting their PhDs exhibited the ability to focus as the determining factor - even though there is a biais right, it would be hard to consider themselves my friends if I didn't consider themselves smart?
Maybe I should spend more time on deciding on what will matter in a year, and less time jumping from toy project to toy project. Maybe I should keep self-commitments even when they are suboptimals.

---

A common advice wholesome higly motivated people that are able to produce qualitative things usually provide, is that you shouldn't compare yourself to others.
I can't disagree more - you should compare yourself in a smart and productive way.

Seeing my father (apparently a recognized expert on what he does) being seen as a genius by some previous students, and knowing him, not having a mental model of how a SPA work is reassuring.
Having the uncle that wanted me to recite the alphabet backward ask me "What do you mean by cardio?" when I described Muay Thai was reassuring.
Having the uncle that doubted my own father cognitive capabilites fall for one of the classic iPhone scams was reassuring.

I'm not trying to overplay the "academincs don't know shit about the real world" common opinion, I'm trying to be quite insistant of the fact that we are all meat bags, sometimes with higher or lower measurable capabilites on specific tests - and that we can perform quite differently under somehow slightly different conditions. The key takeway is that the most important thing if you want to be good at something is to manage the time available to you, accordingly to the interests you are able to focus on, while actually taking into account your current cognitive capabilities in a healthy way.

Each of these anecdotes must not be easy reassurance, but lessons about the value of focus and hard work towards what matters.

Comparing myself to other people not to dwell or reassure yourself, but to grow, is probably the healthiest thing I started doing in the last 5 years.

So, anyway: as a good enough generalist, the biggest challenge for me seems to manage focus, and I'm grateful for the people I was able to observe in their makings once I grew past both the unhealthy comparaisons and the need to stop it altogether, and I hope all this resonates with my fellow underachievers.
